**Abstract** **Background:** Parental health-seeking behavior for illnesses among children is critical for timely and appropriate medical care. This study aimed to investigate the influence of sociodemographic factors on parental health-seeking behavior in Karachi, Pakistan. **Methods:** A cross-sectional study was conducted among 553 parents/caregivers of children aged 0-5 years. Data were collected using a structured questionnaire. Logistic regression analysis was used to determine the associations between sociodemographic factors and parental health-seeking behavior. **Results:** The majority (75.8%) of parents sought professional medical care for their children's illnesses. The odds of seeking professional medical care were significantly higher among parents with higher education levels (adjusted odds ratio [AOR] = 1.94, 95% confidence interval [CI] = 1.13-3.33), higher monthly income (AOR = 1.87, 95% CI = 1.12-3.12), and those who had health insurance (AOR = 2.16, 95% CI = 1.30-3.59). Conversely, the odds were lower among parents who were unemployed (AOR = 0.63, 95% CI = 0.41-0.99). **Conclusions:** Sociodemographic factors, such as education, income, employment status, and health insurance, significantly influence parental health-seeking behavior for pediatric illnesses in Karachi. Targeted interventions are needed to improve health-seeking behavior among disadvantaged populations. **Keywords:** Health-seeking behavior, pediatrics, sociodemographic factors, Karachi, Pakistan

**Abstract** **Background:** Parental health-seeking behavior (HSB) for illnesses among children is crucial for child health outcomes. Sociodemographic factors may influence parental HSB, leading to disparities in child health care utilization. This study aimed to investigate the influence of sociodemographic factors on parental HSB in Karachi, Pakistan. **Methods:** A cross-sectional study was conducted among 1,080 parents of children aged 0-5 years in Karachi. Data were collected through a structured questionnaire administered by trained interviewers. Multiple logistic regression models were used to assess the associations between sociodemographic factors and parental HSB outcomes, including seeking care from a healthcare professional within 24 hours of illness onset and using appropriate treatment for diarrhea and respiratory illnesses. **Results:** The majority of parents (82.5%) sought care within 24 hours of illness onset. However, only 46.2% and 41.8% of parents used appropriate treatment for diarrhea and respiratory illnesses, respectively. Lower education levels (OR = 0.49; 95% CI: 0.34-0.71), lower household income (OR = 0.70; 95% CI: 0.53-0.92), and being a single mother (OR = 0.58; 95% CI: 0.39-0.85) were associated with lower odds of seeking care within 24 hours. Lower education levels were also associated with lower odds of using appropriate treatment for both diarrhea (OR = 0.59; 95% CI: 0.42-0.83) and respiratory illnesses (OR = 0.63; 95% CI: 0.44-0.90). **Conclusion:** Sociodemographic factors, particularly education level, household income, and parental status, play a significant role in parental HSB for childhood illnesses in Karachi. Interventions targeting these factors are needed to improve child health outcomes and reduce health disparities.
